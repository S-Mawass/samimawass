Case Study

Higher Education · UAE · Jan 2025–May 2026

Academic Resource Planning
& Faculty Capacity

Power BI faculty capacity and academic resource planning dashboard for a UAE national higher education institution — providing department heads and academic planners with a live view of ~320 faculty workloads, teaching hours, and resource gaps.

Problem

The institution's academic planning process relied on manual teaching load spreadsheets managed by individual departments. With ~320 faculty members spanning multiple colleges and disciplines, there was no consolidated view of workload distribution, over-allocation risk, or semester-by-semester capacity gaps. Department heads had no visibility into peer departments, making cross-faculty resource sharing or emergency cover planning reactive and difficult to coordinate. Academic administrators faced a significant data assembly task before each semester planning cycle.

Approach

Built as part of the broader institutional analytics platform. Designed a data model integrating faculty profile data, course allocation records, contact hours, and semester planning targets. Power Query transformations standardised course load units across departments with different calculation methodologies. Wrote DAX measures for total contact hours per faculty, utilisation percentage against contracted capacity, teaching load balance scores, and department-level aggregate capacity gaps. Implemented row-level security so each department administrator saw only their faculty, while senior academic planners had an institution-wide view. Delivered views for: Faculty Workload Summary, Department Capacity, Over-Allocation Risk Board, and Semester Planning Comparison.

Key Outcome

Senior academic leadership gained the institution's first cross-college view of faculty utilisation — revealing systematic over-allocation in two colleges that had been invisible in the fragmented department-level spreadsheets. The semester planning comparison view reduced the time academic planners spent preparing for resource allocation meetings by 30%. The over-allocation risk board enabled proactive intervention — identifying faculty approaching unsustainable workloads before the semester began rather than reacting to complaints at mid-semester review.
