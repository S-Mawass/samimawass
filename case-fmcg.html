Problem

Two hours per shift lost to manual Excel reporting.

At the start of every shift, an FMCG manufacturer in Scotland had no automated view of what was happening on the production floor. Operators and supervisors spent approximately two hours manually pulling data from production systems into Excel, formatting it, and distributing reports before any analysis could begin.

By the time a report reached the finance or operations team, the shift was already underway. Decisions about downtime response, waste thresholds, and production efficiency were made on figures that were always hours behind.

OEE — Overall Equipment Effectiveness — was calculated manually and inconsistently. Different people applied different formulas. The number meant different things to different teams.

Context

SQL Server source, three stakeholder audiences, one floor.

Production data lived in SQL Server, already captured by existing plant systems. The data was there — the problem was getting it to the right people, in the right shape, fast enough to be useful.

Three distinct audiences needed different views of the same data: operations needed real-time OEE and downtime to act on; quality and line management needed waste tracking; finance needed shift-level financial performance against targets.

Approach & Build

SQL models → automated refresh → audience-specific dashboards.

  • SQL reporting models — views and calculated fields for OEE (availability × performance × quality), downtime categorisation, waste by line and product, and shift financial summaries. One agreed OEE formula, defined once.
  • Power BI dashboards — four audience-specific dashboards built on a single Power BI dataset: real-time OEE for operations, downtime root-cause view for line management, waste tracking for quality, and shift financial performance for finance.
  • Automated scheduled refresh — replaced the manual Excel pull entirely. Reports are current at the start of every shift without any manual intervention.

Outcome

~2 hours/shift recovered. Real-time OEE on the floor.

  • Approximately two hours of manual reporting work per shift was eliminated. The time was recovered by operations and finance teams for actual analysis and decision-making.
  • OEE is now calculated from a single agreed formula, consistently, across every report. The number means the same thing in every meeting.
  • Finance has shift-level financial data available at the start of the shift — not hours later after manual compilation.
  • Downtime and waste patterns that were previously invisible in aggregate became actionable: teams could see which lines or products drove the most downtime or waste over time.

Skills & Analytics Engineering Relevance

The pipeline matters as much as the report.

This engagement made clear that the delivery mechanism — automated refresh replacing manual extraction — was as valuable as the dashboard design. The best report in the world is useless if the data flowing into it is two hours stale and requires a human to compile it.

This is a core analytics engineering principle: automation of the data pipeline is not a nice-to-have, it's what makes analytics reliable. The SQL models built here — defining OEE, categorising downtime, structuring shift financials — are exactly what dbt models do in a modern stack: single source of truth for business logic, version-controlled, reusable across any downstream report.
